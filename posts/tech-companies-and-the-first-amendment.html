Jared Culp | 2021-01-12

Tech companies and the first amendment

Before I get started, a bit of background: I've always wanted to blog in some capacity. This is my mediocre attempt at that. I doubt many people will read it, but I hope to improve my writing and communication skills along the way. I did not particularly envision my first post being political, but this is what I feel compelled to write about this morning.

Just when we thought 2020 was enough, 2021 has reared its ugly head, exposing the underlying political divide rooted deep in everyday discourse. In particular, after last week's terrifying events at the capital, the issue of the first amendment and free speech on the Internet has come into the spotlight. As I listened to the talking pundits and read the questionable at best hot-takes on Twitter, I felt the need to express my opinion on the topic.

As any person with a pulse is aware, Twitter permanently suspended President Trump's Twitter account. This coincides with a string of take downs by Silicon Valley, as AWS removed the Parler social network from its servers, shutting down its entire network in the blink of an eye. This has raised some worthwhile questions around the role that large tech companies should play in public discourse. My issue is that that isn't the debate that's actually happening. Instead, we've gotten bogged down by fallacious arguments implying that the President (and his supporters) have been silenced.

I'm not sure who exactly needs to hear this—and let's be honest, those people aren't the ones (if any) who are going to read this—but I honestly cannot comprehend how this is even a debate. Let's focus on Twitter and the President. Nobody (myself included) reads the end user license agreement when they sign up for online services. Let me sum it up for you: you are signing up for a free service, subject to the service operator's terms and conditions. So when Twitter deems that the President's words have tangible and potentially life-endangering impact, they are completely within their rights to terminate his account.

In case that's too abstract, imagine you are a restaurant owner. It's a typical Tuesday evening and you've got a few customers minding their own business. All of a sudden, a patron arrives spewing obscenities, which negatively impacts the experience the rest of your customers have. While some would argue this person is well within their rights of the first amendment to say whatever is on their mind...would those same people object to you, the owner of the restaurant, asking this person to leave? And if that person refuses to leave, would they object to you forcing them out by calling law enforcement or using an in-house security employee?

Critics have said that a handful of tech CEOs have effectively "silenced" President Trump. I call bullshit. Every single President before him managed to perform their duties without the use of social media. If he truly felt silenced, he could get on the phone (literally at any time or place), ask to hold a press conference, and every single major news outlet would preempt their ongoing programming to broadcast it on national TV.

Those arguing that their first amendment rights are being taken away need to retake a high-school course in civics, or at the very least take a reflective look in the mirror. The Constitution endows you with the rights of free speech. It does not endow upon you impunity from consequences or criticism of that free speech. In this particular case, Twitter's suspension of President Trump's account was long overdue, after four years of enabling him to spew his divisive bullshit and lies, while in the process enabling stochastic domestic terrorism. Even if that weren't the case, they are still well within their legal rights to terminate his access to their platform, for any reason they deem necessary (which they don't need to defend, by the way).

Let's stop ringing the alarm bells. This is not Orwell's 1984.
